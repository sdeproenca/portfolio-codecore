Hi, I'm Sofi. Argentinian and Italian, working nomad. Eight-plus years in product, program, and operations management. I've been called project manager, program manager, product manager, product owner. The truth is I'm the person who sees a problem and can't help but solve it.

I build the operating systems that let teams ship predictably. Blockchain infrastructure, ecommerce, public-sector tech, pharma marketing, enterprise data. The domain changes. The pattern doesn't.

## The arc

I came up through international relations and public policy, and joined the Buenos Aires Ministry of Justice in 2017. By 2019 I was project manager on one of the largest public security technology programs in Argentina in the last decade. Walking in, there was no operations playbook anywhere in the building. There rarely was, across government back then. What there was: high-stakes public infrastructure, judicial oversight built into the daily workflow, multiple ministries and agencies that had to stay aligned, law enforcement on the ground, and the kind of compliance demands you only get when a system touches people's lives directly. I learned how to build operating systems where none existed, because the alternative was that nothing would land.

That project gave me the question I have been answering ever since: how do you make a technically ambitious thing land on time, under oversight, when the stakes are real and half the team is in a different timezone?

Around the same time, in 2018, I first learned what Bitcoin was. I was hooked instantly. The economic case made instant sense in Argentina. But the bigger pull was what it pointed at: centralization, what it costs, and what technology can do to put power back in people's hands. From then on, I wanted my work in service of that.

The years since have been variations on the same question, and the through-line has held. There is a moment in every job where someone says the thing cannot be done on the timeline they need it done. A cross-chain product six weeks out from a live demo on a conference stage. An ecommerce agency carrying more clients than its coordination could hold. A blockchain operating system shipping monthly across five timezones with twelve chain integrations inside one of those months. In every case my job was to disagree with that moment, in detail, with a plan. Then to build the operating system that made the plan true. The reason I keep finding myself in rooms like these is that the technology in them, when it actually ships, is the kind that gives people more agency than they had before. More control over their money, their tools, their work, their attention. The operations layer is what makes that real. So I keep building it.
